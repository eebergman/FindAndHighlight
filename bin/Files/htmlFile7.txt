     <p><b>Gloria Laura Vanderbilt</b> (born February 20, 1924) is an American artist, author, actress, <a href="/wiki/Beneficiary" title="Beneficiary">heiress</a> and <a href="/wiki/Socialite" title="Socialite">socialite</a>. During the 1930s, she was the subject of a high-profile <a href="/wiki/Child_custody_laws_in_the_United_States" title="Child custody laws in the United States">child custody trial</a> in which her mother, <a href="/wiki/Gloria_Morgan_Vanderbilt" title="Gloria Morgan Vanderbilt">Gloria Morgan Vanderbilt</a>, and her paternal aunt, <a href="/wiki/Gertrude_Vanderbilt_Whitney" title="Gertrude Vanderbilt Whitney">Gertrude Vanderbilt Whitney</a>, both sought custody of the child Gloria and control over her $5 million trust fund. The trial, called the "<a href="/wiki/Trial_of_the_century" title="Trial of the century">trial of the century</a>" by the press, was the subject of wide and sensational press coverage due to the wealth and notoriety of the involved parties and the scandalous evidence presented to support Whitney's claim that Gloria Morgan Vanderbilt was an unfit parent.</p> 
     <p>As an adult in the 1970s, Gloria Laura Vanderbilt became known in connection with a line of fashions, perfumes and household goods bearing her name. She was particularly noted as an early developer of designer <a href="/wiki/Jeans" title="Jeans">blue jeans</a>.</p> 
     <p>She is a member of the <a href="/wiki/Vanderbilt_family" title="Vanderbilt family">Vanderbilt family of New York</a> and the mother of <a href="/wiki/CNN" title="CNN">CNN</a> television anchor <a href="/wiki/Anderson_Cooper" title="Anderson Cooper">Anderson Cooper</a>.</p>

     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gloria_Vanderbilt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>Vanderbilt was born on February 20, 1924, in New York City, the only child of railroad heir <a href="/wiki/Reginald_Claypoole_Vanderbilt" title="Reginald Claypoole Vanderbilt">Reginald Claypoole Vanderbilt</a> (1880–1925)<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">[1]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Reggie_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Reggie-2">[2]</a></sup> and his second wife, <a href="/wiki/Gloria_Morgan_Vanderbilt" title="Gloria Morgan Vanderbilt">Gloria Morgan</a> (1904–1965).<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">[3]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> When Gloria was born, her father was heard to delightedly exclaim "It is fantastic how Vanderbilt she looks! See the corners of her eyes, how they turn up?"<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup> She was baptized in the <a href="/wiki/Episcopal_Church_USA" title="Episcopal Church USA" class="mw-redirect">Episcopal church</a> by Bishop <a href="/wiki/Herbert_Shipman" title="Herbert Shipman">Herbert Shipman</a> as Gloria Laura Vanderbilt. After her father's death, she was <a href="/wiki/Confirmation" title="Confirmation">confirmed</a> and raised in the <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">Catholic Church</a>, to which her mother belonged.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> From her father's first marriage, to Cathleen Neilson, she had a half-sister, Cathleen Vanderbilt (1904–1944).<sup id="cite_ref-Death_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Death-7">[7]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>She became heiress to a half share in a $5 million trust fund upon her father's death from <a href="/wiki/Cirrhosis" title="Cirrhosis">cirrhosis</a> when she was 18 months old.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The rights to control this trust fund while Vanderbilt was a minor belonged to her mother, who traveled to and from Paris for years, taking her daughter with her. They were accompanied by a beloved nanny - Emma Sullivan Kieslich,<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup> whom young Gloria had named "Dodo" - who would play a tumultuous part in the child's life,<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> and her mother's identical twin sister, <a href="/wiki/Thelma_Furness,_Viscountess_Furness" title="Thelma Furness, Viscountess Furness">Thelma</a>, who was the <a href="/wiki/Mistress_(lover)" title="Mistress (lover)">mistress</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Edward_VIII_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Edward VIII of the United Kingdom" class="mw-redirect">Prince of Wales</a> during this time.<sup id="cite_ref-Happy_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Happy-11">[11]</a></sup> As a result of frequent spending, her mother's use of finances was scrutinized by the child Vanderbilt's paternal aunt, Gertrude Vanderbilt Whitney. A sculptor and philanthropist, Whitney wanted custody of her niece, which resulted in a famous custody trial.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">[12]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup> The trial was so scandalous that at times, the judge would make everyone leave the room so as to listen to what young Vanderbilt had to say without anyone influencing her. Some people heard weeping and wailing inside the court room. Testimony was heard depicting the mother as an unfit parent; Vanderbilt's mother lost the battle and Vanderbilt became the ward of her aunt Gertrude.<sup id="cite_ref-Happy_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Happy-11">[11]</a></sup></p>

     <p>Litigation continued, however. Vanderbilt's mother was forced to live on a drastically reduced portion of her daughter's trust, which was worth more than $4 million at the end of 1937<sup id="cite_ref-life1938011717_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-life1938011717-14">[14]</a></sup> ($65.2 million in 2014 dollars<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup>). Visitation was also closely watched to ensure that Vanderbilt's mother did not exert any undue influence upon her daughter with her supposedly "raucous" lifestyle. Vanderbilt was raised amidst luxury at her aunt Gertrude's mansion in Old Westbury, Long Island, surrounded by cousins her age who lived in houses circling the vast estate, and in New York City.</p> 
     <p>The story of the trial was told in a 1982 miniseries for NBC <i><a href="/wiki/Little_Gloria..._Happy_at_Last" title="Little Gloria... Happy at Last">Little Gloria... Happy at Last</a></i>, which was nominated for six Emmys and a Golden Globe.</p> 
     <p>Vanderbilt attended the Greenvale School on <a href="/wiki/Long_Island" title="Long Island">Long Island</a>; <a href="/wiki/Miss_Porter%27s_School" title="Miss Porter's School">Miss Porter's School</a> in <a href="/wiki/Farmington,_Connecticut" title="Farmington, Connecticut">Farmington, Connecticut</a>; and then the <a href="/wiki/Wheeler_School" title="Wheeler School">Wheeler School</a><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> in <a href="/wiki/Providence,_Rhode_Island" title="Providence, Rhode Island">Providence, Rhode Island</a>, as well as the Art Students League in New York City, developing the artistic talent for which she would become increasingly known in her career. When Vanderbilt came of age and took control of her trust fund, she cut her mother off entirely,<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> though she supported her in later years.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Her mother lived for many years with her sister, Thelma, Lady Furness, in Beverly Hills and died there in 1965.</p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Professional_career">Professional career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gloria_Vanderbilt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Professional career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>Vanderbilt studied acting at the Neighborhood Playhouse with teacher Sanford Meisner and studied art at the <a href="/wiki/Art_Students_League_of_New_York" title="Art Students League of New York">Art Students League of New York</a>. She became known for her artwork, giving one-woman shows of oil paintings, watercolors, and pastels. This artwork was adapted and licensed, starting about 1968, by <a href="/wiki/Hallmark_Cards" title="Hallmark Cards">Hallmark Cards</a> (a manufacturer of paper products) and by Bloomcraft (a textile manufacturer), and Vanderbilt began designing specifically for linens, pottery, and glassware.</p> 
     <p>From 1954 through 1963 Vanderbilt applied herself to acting, appearing in a number of live and filmed television dramas including <a href="/wiki/Playhouse_90" title="Playhouse 90">Playhouse 90</a>, <a href="/wiki/Studio_One_in_Hollywood" title="Studio One in Hollywood" class="mw-redirect">Studio One in Hollywood</a>, and <a href="/wiki/The_Dick_Powell_Show" title="The Dick Powell Show">The Dick Powell Show</a>. She also made an appearance in a two part episode of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Love_Boat" title="The Love Boat">The Love Boat</a></i> in 1981. Other TV programs on which she appeared include <i><a href="/wiki/Person_to_Person" title="Person to Person">Person to Person</a></i> with <a href="/wiki/Edward_R._Murrow" title="Edward R. Murrow">Edward R. Murrow</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Tonight_Show_Starring_Johnny_Carson" title="The Tonight Show Starring Johnny Carson">The Tonight Show Starring Johnny Carson</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Oprah_Winfrey_Show" title="The Oprah Winfrey Show">The Oprah Winfrey Show</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Live!_with_Kelly_and_Michael" title="Live! with Kelly and Michael">Live! with Kelly and Michael</a></i>, and <i>CBS News Sunday Morning</i>.</p> 
     <p>Vanderbilt was also a top international fashion model. Beginning with the custody trial during her childhood, appearing at age 17 in <i><a href="/wiki/Harper%27s_Bazaar" title="Harper's Bazaar">Harper's Bazaar</a></i>, being the public face of her clothing and fragrances lines, and noted for inspiring <a href="/wiki/Richard_Avedon" title="Richard Avedon">Richard Avedon</a>, she has been a popular subject for photographers her entire life.</p> 
     <p>During the 1970s, she ventured into the fashion business, first with Glentex, licensing her name for a line of scarves. In 1976, Indian designer Mohan Murjani's Murjani Corporation, proposed launching a line of <a href="/wiki/Designer_jeans" title="Designer jeans" class="mw-redirect">designer jeans</a> carrying Vanderbilt's signature embroidered on the back pocket, as well as her swan logo. Her jeans were more tightly fitted than other jeans of that time. The logo eventually appeared on dresses and perfumes while Vanderbilt also launched a line of blouses, sheets, shoes, leather goods, liqueurs, and accessories. Vanderbilt was one of the first designers to make public appearances, which was a difficult thing for her because of her shyness.</p> 
     <p>In 1978, Gloria Vanderbilt sold the rights to her name to the Murjani Group.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> She then launched her own company, "GV Ltd.," on 7th Avenue in New York. In the period from 1982 to 2002 <a href="/wiki/L%27Oreal" title="L'Oreal" class="mw-redirect">L'Oreal</a> launched eight fragrances under the brand name <i>Gloria Vanderbilt</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/The_Jones_Group" title="The Jones Group" class="mw-redirect">Jones Apparel Group</a> acquired the rights to Gloria Vanderbilt jeans in 2002.</p> 
     <p>In the 1980s, Vanderbilt accused her former partners in GV Ltd. and her lawyer of fraud. After a lengthy trial (during which time the lawyer died) Vanderbilt won and was awarded nearly $1.7 million, but the money was never recovered, though she was also awarded $300,000 by the New York Bar Association from its Victims of Fraud fund. Vanderbilt owed millions in back taxes—the lawyer had never paid the IRS—and she was forced to sell her Southampton and New York City homes.</p> 
     <p>In 2001, Vanderbilt opened her first art exhibition, "Dream Boxes," at the Southern Vermont Arts Center in Manchester. A critical success, she launched another exhibition of 35 paintings at the Arts Center in 2007. Two years later, she returned to the Arts Center as a panelist at its Annual Fall Show Exhibition, signing copies of her latest novel, "Obsession: An Erotic Tale."</p> 
     <p>Vanderbilt is the author of four memoirs and three novels, and is a regular contributor to <i>The New York Times</i>, <i>Vanity Fair</i> and <i>Elle</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> In November 2010, Vanderbilt was the subject of a new book chronicling her life, entitled <i>The World of Gloria Vanderbilt,</i><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> written by Wendy Goodman, <a href="/wiki/New_York_(magazine)" title="New York (magazine)"><i>New York</i></a>'s design editor. The book was published by Abrams and features many previously unreleased photographs. Vanderbilt also has a website featuring her artwork.</p> 
     <p>On April 5, 2016, HarperCollins Publishers released a new book by Vanderbilt and her son <a href="/wiki/Anderson_Cooper" title="Anderson Cooper">Anderson Cooper</a>, <i>The Rainbow Comes and Goes: A Mother and Son On Life, Love, and Loss</i>. The book is described as "A charming and intimate collection of correspondence between #1 New York Times bestselling author Anderson Cooper and his mother, Gloria Vanderbilt, that offers timeless wisdom and a revealing glimpse into their lives."<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>On April 9, 2016, HBO premiered <i>Nothing Left Unsaid: Gloria Vanderbilt &amp; Anderson Cooper</i>, a two-hour documentary produced and directed by <a href="/wiki/Liz_Garbus" title="Liz Garbus">Liz Garbus</a> that features a series of conversations between the mother and son, covering the mother's storied life and family history in the public eye.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup></p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gloria_Vanderbilt&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>Vanderbilt was raised <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic" title="Roman Catholic" class="mw-redirect">Roman Catholic</a> and as a child was particularly fascinated with <a href="/wiki/St._Theresa" title="St. Theresa" class="mw-redirect">St. Theresa</a>. Although religious in her youth, she no longer practices Catholicism and identifies more with a <a href="/wiki/Zen_Buddhism" title="Zen Buddhism" class="mw-redirect">Zen Buddhism</a> ideology.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>At age 17, Vanderbilt went to Hollywood where she married agent <a href="/wiki/Pat_DiCicco" title="Pat DiCicco">Pat DiCicco</a> in 1941;<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">[27]</a></sup> they divorced in 1945.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> DiCicco proved to be temperamental and an abusive husband who called her 'Fatsy Roo' and beat her. 'He would take my head and bang it against the wall,' Vanderbilt said. 'I had black eyes.'<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">[29]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>Her second marriage, to conductor <a href="/wiki/Leopold_Stokowski" title="Leopold Stokowski">Leopold Stokowski</a> in April 1945, produced two sons before their divorce in October 1955:</p> 
     <ul> 
      <li>Leopold Stanislaus "Stan" Stokowski (born August 22, 1950), who married Ivy Strick, an author, and later, Emily Goldstein, an artist<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup> 
       <ul> 
        <li>Aurora Stokowski (born in March 1983) (with Ivy Strick), who married Anthony Mazzei in June 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-AuroraWedd_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AuroraWedd-31">[31]</a></sup></li> 
        <li>Abra Stokowski (born in February 1985) (with Ivy Strick)</li> 
        <li>Myles Stokowski (born in 1998) (with Emily Goldstein)</li> 
       </ul> </li> 
      <li>Christopher Stokowski (born January 31, 1952)</li> 
     </ul> 
     <p>On August 28, 1956, she married director <a href="/wiki/Sidney_Lumet" title="Sidney Lumet">Sidney Lumet</a>; they divorced in August 1963.</p> 
     <p>She married her fourth husband, author <a href="/wiki/Wyatt_Emory_Cooper" title="Wyatt Emory Cooper">Wyatt Emory Cooper</a> on December 24, 1963. They had two sons:</p> 
     <ul> 
      <li>Carter Vanderbilt Cooper (January 27, 1965&nbsp;– July 22, 1988)</li> 
      <li><a href="/wiki/Anderson_Cooper" title="Anderson Cooper">Anderson Hays Cooper</a> (born June 3, 1967), <a href="/wiki/CNN" title="CNN">CNN</a> news anchor</li> 
     </ul> 
     <p>Wyatt Cooper died in 1978 during open heart surgery in New York City. Carter Cooper committed suicide at the age of 23 by jumping from the family's 14th-floor apartment.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">[32]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>She maintained a romantic relationship with photographer and filmmaker <a href="/wiki/Gordon_Parks" title="Gordon Parks">Gordon Parks</a> for many years until his death in 2006.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Other notable lovers have included <a href="/wiki/Marlon_Brando" title="Marlon Brando">Marlon Brando</a>, <a href="/wiki/Frank_Sinatra" title="Frank Sinatra">Frank Sinatra</a>, <a href="/wiki/Howard_Hughes" title="Howard Hughes">Howard Hughes</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Roald_Dahl" title="Roald Dahl">Roald Dahl</a>. <a href="/wiki/Truman_Capote" title="Truman Capote">Truman Capote</a> was said to have modeled the character of Holly Golightly in <i><a href="/wiki/Breakfast_at_Tiffany%27s_(novella)" title="Breakfast at Tiffany's (novella)">Breakfast at Tiffany's</a></i> on her, but others say it was based on her friend <a href="/wiki/Carol_Grace" title="Carol Grace">Carol Grace</a>.</p> 
     <p>Vanderbilt is very close friends with fashion designer <a href="/wiki/Diane_von_F%C3%BCrstenberg" title="Diane von Fürstenberg">Diane von Fürstenberg</a> and comedian <a href="/wiki/Kathy_Griffin" title="Kathy Griffin">Kathy Griffin</a>. While appearing as a guest on her son <a href="/wiki/Anderson_Cooper" title="Anderson Cooper">Anderson Cooper</a>'s television talk show, <a href="/wiki/Anderson_(TV_series)" title="Anderson (TV series)" class="mw-redirect"><i>Anderson</i></a> on September 19, 2011, Vanderbilt referred to Griffin as her "fantasy daughter".<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>When Vanderbilt celebrated her 90th birthday on February 20, 2014, her collections of many drawings, paintings and collectibles were placed on display in the 1stdibs Gallery at <a href="/w/index.php?title=New_York_Design_Center&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="New York Design Center (page does not exist)">New York Design Center</a> in New York City.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p>
