     <p>The <b>1896 Summer Olympics</b> (<a href="/wiki/Modern_Greek_language" title="Modern Greek language" class="mw-redirect">Modern Greek</a>: <span lang="ell" xml:lang="ell">??????? ?????????? ?????? 1896</span>, <span title="Greek transliteration" class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none"><i>Therinoí Olympiakoí Agó?nes 1896</i></span>), officially known as the <b>Games of the I Olympiad</b>, was a <a href="/wiki/Multi-sport_event" title="Multi-sport event">multi-sport event</a> held in <a href="/wiki/Athens" title="Athens">Athens</a>, Greece, from 6 to 15 April 1896. It was the first international Olympic Games held in the <a href="/wiki/Modern_era" title="Modern era" class="mw-redirect">modern era</a>. Because <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">Ancient Greece</a> was the birthplace of the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Olympic_Games" title="Ancient Olympic Games">Olympic Games</a>, Athens was considered to be an appropriate choice to stage the inaugural modern Games. It was unanimously chosen as the host city during a congress organised by <a href="/wiki/Pierre_de_Coubertin" title="Pierre de Coubertin">Pierre de Coubertin</a>, a French <a href="/wiki/Pedagogy" title="Pedagogy">pedagogue</a> and historian, in Paris, on 23 June 1894. The <a href="/wiki/International_Olympic_Committee" title="International Olympic Committee">International Olympic Committee</a> (IOC) was also instituted during this congress.</p> 
     <p>Despite many obstacles and setbacks, the 1896 Olympics were regarded as a great success. The Games had the largest international participation of any sporting event to that date. The <a href="/wiki/Panathenaic_Stadium" title="Panathenaic Stadium">Panathinaiko Stadium</a>, the only Olympic stadium used in the 1800s, overflowed with the largest crowd ever to watch a sporting event.<sup id="cite_ref-Y153_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Y153-3">[3]</a></sup> The highlight for the Greeks was the <a href="/wiki/Marathon" title="Marathon">marathon</a> victory by their compatriot <a href="/wiki/Spyridon_Louis" title="Spyridon Louis">Spyridon Louis</a>. The most successful competitor was German <a href="/wiki/Sport_wrestling" title="Sport wrestling" class="mw-redirect">wrestler</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gymnastics" title="Gymnastics">gymnast</a> <a href="/wiki/Carl_Schuhmann" title="Carl Schuhmann">Carl Schuhmann</a>, who won four events.</p> 
     <p>After the Games, Coubertin and the IOC were petitioned by several prominent figures, including Greece's <a href="/wiki/George_I_of_Greece" title="George I of Greece">King George</a> and some of the American competitors in Athens, to hold all the following Games in Athens. However, the <a href="/wiki/1900_Summer_Olympics" title="1900 Summer Olympics">1900 Summer Olympics</a> were already planned for Paris and, except for the <a href="/wiki/1906_Intercalated_Games" title="1906 Intercalated Games">Intercalated Games of 1906</a>, the Olympics did not return to Greece until the <a href="/wiki/2004_Summer_Olympics" title="2004 Summer Olympics">2004 Summer Olympics</a>, 108 years later.</p>

     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reviving_the_Games">Reviving the Games</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1896_Summer_Olympics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Reviving the Games">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>During the 19th century, several small-scale sports festivals across Europe were named after the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Olympic_Games" title="Ancient Olympic Games">Ancient Olympic Games</a>. The 1870 <a href="/wiki/Zappas_Olympics" title="Zappas Olympics">Olympics</a> at the Panathenaic stadium, which had been refurbished for the occasion, had an audience of 30,000 people.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">[4]</a></sup> Coubertin adopted Dr <a href="/wiki/William_Penny_Brookes" title="William Penny Brookes">William Penny Brookes</a>' idea to establish a multi-national and <a href="/wiki/Multi-sport_event" title="Multi-sport event">multi-sport event</a>—the ancient games only allowed free male athletes of Greek origin to participate.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">[5]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">[6]</a></sup> In 1890, Coubertin wrote an article in <i>La Revue Athletique</i>, which espoused the importance of <a href="/wiki/Much_Wenlock" title="Much Wenlock">Much Wenlock</a>—a rural market town in the English county of <a href="/wiki/Shropshire" title="Shropshire">Shropshire</a>. It was here that, in October 1850, the local physician William Penny Brookes had founded the <a href="/wiki/Wenlock_Olympian_Society_Annual_Games" title="Wenlock Olympian Society Annual Games" class="mw-redirect">Wenlock Olympian Games</a>, a festival of sports and recreations that included athletics and team sports, such as <a href="/wiki/Cricket" title="Cricket">cricket</a>, <a href="/wiki/Association_football" title="Association football">football</a> and <a href="/wiki/Quoits" title="Quoits">quoits</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">[7]</a></sup> Coubertin also took inspiration from the earlier Greek games organised under the name of <a href="/wiki/Zappas_Olympics" title="Zappas Olympics">Olympics</a> by businessman and philanthropist <a href="/wiki/Evangelis_Zappas" title="Evangelis Zappas" class="mw-redirect">Evangelis Zappas</a> in 1859, 1870 and 1875.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">[8]</a></sup> The 1896 Athens Games was funded by the legacies of <a href="/wiki/Evangelis_Zappas" title="Evangelis Zappas" class="mw-redirect">Evangelis Zappas</a> and his cousin <a href="/wiki/Konstantinos_Zappas" title="Konstantinos Zappas">Konstantinos Zappas</a><sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">[9]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup> and by <a href="/wiki/George_Averoff" title="George Averoff">George Averoff</a><sup id="cite_ref-Young_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Young-12">[12]</a></sup> who had been specifically requested by the Greek government, through crown prince Constantine, to sponsor the second refurbishment of the <a href="/wiki/Panathenaic_Stadium" title="Panathenaic Stadium">Panathinaiko Stadium</a>. This the Greek government did despite the fact that the cost of refurbishing the stadium in marble had already been funded in full by Evangelis Zappas forty years earlier.<sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">[13]</a></sup></p> 
     <blockquote class="templatequote"> 
      <p>With deep feeling towards Baron de Coubertin's courteous petition, I send him and the members of the Congress, with my sincere thanks, my best wishes for the revival of the Olympic Games.</p> 
      <div class="templatequotecite">
       <cite>—?<a href="/wiki/George_I_of_Greece" title="George I of Greece">King George</a> of Greece (21 June 1894)<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup></cite>
      </div> 
     </blockquote> 
     <p>On 18 June 1894, Coubertin organised a congress at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Paris" title="University of Paris">Sorbonne</a>, Paris, to present his plans to representatives of sports societies from 11 countries. Following his proposal's acceptance by the congress, a date for the first modern Olympic Games needed to be chosen. Coubertin suggested that the Games be held concurrently with the 1900 <a href="/wiki/World%27s_Fair" title="World's Fair" class="mw-redirect">Universal Exposition</a> of Paris. Concerned that a six-year waiting period might lessen public interest, congress members opted instead to hold the inaugural Games in 1896. With a date established, members of the congress turned their attention to the selection of a host city. It remains a mystery how Athens was finally chosen to host the inaugural Games. In the following years both Coubertin and <a href="/wiki/Demetrius_Vikelas" title="Demetrius Vikelas">Demetrius Vikelas</a> would offer recollections of the selection process that contradicted the official minutes of the congress. Most accounts hold that several congressmen first proposed London as the location, but Coubertin dissented. After a brief discussion with Vikelas, who represented Greece, Coubertin suggested Athens. Vikelas made the Athens proposal official on 23 June, and since Greece had been the original home of the Olympics, the congress unanimously approved the decision. Vikelas was then elected the first president of the newly established <a href="/wiki/International_Olympic_Committee" title="International Olympic Committee">International Olympic Committee</a> (IOC).<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup></p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Organization">Organization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1896_Summer_Olympics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Organization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

     <p>News that the Olympic Games would return to Greece was well received by the Greek public, media, and <a href="/wiki/Royal_Family_of_Greece" title="Royal Family of Greece" class="mw-redirect">royal family</a>. According to Coubertin, "the Crown Prince <a href="/wiki/Constantine_I_of_Greece" title="Constantine I of Greece">Constantine</a> learned with great pleasure that the Games will be inaugurated in Athens." Coubertin went on to confirm that, "the King and the Crown Prince will confer their patronage on the holding of these games." Constantine later conferred more than that; he eagerly assumed the presidency of the 1896 organising committee.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>However, the country had financial troubles and was in political turmoil. The job of <a href="/wiki/List_of_Prime_Ministers_of_Greece" title="List of Prime Ministers of Greece">prime minister</a> alternated between <a href="/wiki/Charilaos_Trikoupis" title="Charilaos Trikoupis">Charilaos Trikoupis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Theodoros_Deligiannis" title="Theodoros Deligiannis">Theodoros Deligiannis</a> frequently during the last years of the 19th century. Because of this financial and political instability, both prime minister Trikoupis and <a href="/wiki/Stephanos_Dragoumis" title="Stephanos Dragoumis" class="mw-redirect">Stephanos Dragoumis</a>, the president of the Zappas Olympic Committee, which had attempted to organise a series of national Olympiads, believed that Greece could not host the event.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> In late 1894, the organising committee under <a href="/wiki/Stephanos_Skouloudis" title="Stephanos Skouloudis">Stephanos Skouloudis</a> presented a report that the cost of the Games would be three times higher than originally estimated by Coubertin. They concluded the Games could not be held, and offered their resignation. The total cost of the Games was 3,740,000 gold <a href="/wiki/Drachma" title="Drachma" class="mw-redirect">drachmas</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Zarnowski_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Zarnowski-18">[18]</a></sup></p>

        <a href="/wiki/Demetrius_Vikelas" title="Demetrius Vikelas">Demetrius Vikelas</a>, the first president of the International Olympic Committee, was credited with the successful organisation of the 1896 Games
       </div> 
      </div> 
     </div> 
     <p>With the prospect of reviving the Olympic games very much in doubt, Coubertin and Vikelas commenced a campaign to keep the Olympic movement alive. Their efforts culminated on 7 January 1895 when Vikelas announced that crown prince Constantine would assume the presidency of the organising committee. His first responsibility was to raise the funds necessary to host the Games. He relied on the patriotism of the Greek people to motivate them to provide the required finances.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Constantine's enthusiasm sparked a wave of contributions from the Greek public. This grassroots effort raised 330,000 drachmas. A special set of postage stamps were commissioned; the sale of which raised 400,000 drachmas. Ticket sales added 200,000 drachmas. At the request of Constantine, businessman <a href="/wiki/George_Averoff" title="George Averoff">George Averoff</a> agreed to pay for the restoration of the Panathinaiko Stadium. Averoff would donate 920,000 drachmas<sup id="cite_ref-Young_12-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Young-12">[12]</a></sup> to this project.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> As a tribute to his generosity, a statue of Averoff was constructed and unveiled on 5 April 1896 outside the stadium. It stands there to this day.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>Some of the athletes would take part in the Games because they happened to be in Athens at the time the Games were held, either on holiday or for work (e.g., some of the British competitors worked for the British <a href="/wiki/Diplomatic_mission" title="Diplomatic mission">embassy</a>). A designated <a href="/wiki/Olympic_Village" title="Olympic Village">Olympic Village</a> for the athletes did not appear until the <a href="/wiki/1932_Summer_Olympics" title="1932 Summer Olympics">1932 Summer Olympics</a>. Consequently, the athletes had to provide their own lodging.</p>

     </div> 
     <p>The first regulation voted on by the new IOC in 1894 was to allow only amateur athletes to participate in the Olympic Games.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup> The various contests were thus held under amateur regulations with the exception of fencing matches.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23">[23]</a></sup> The rules and regulations were not uniform, so the Organising Committee had to choose among the codes of the various national athletic associations. The jury, the referees and the game director bore the same names as in antiquity (<i>Ephor</i>, <i>Helanodic</i> and <i>Alitarc</i>). <a href="/wiki/Prince_George_of_Greece" title="Prince George of Greece" class="mw-redirect">Prince George</a> acted as final referee; according to Coubertin, "his presence gave weight and authority to the decisions of the ephors."<sup id="cite_ref-C46-47_24-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-C46-47-24">[24]</a></sup></p>

     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Opening_ceremony">Opening ceremony</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1896_Summer_Olympics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Opening ceremony">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <div class="thumb tright"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:1896_Olympic_opening_ceremony.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/1896_Olympic_opening_ceremony.jpg/220px-1896_Olympic_opening_ceremony.jpg" width="220" height="172" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/b6/1896_Olympic_opening_ceremony.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/b6/1896_Olympic_opening_ceremony.jpg 2x" data-file-width="314" data-file-height="245"></a> 
       <div class="thumbcaption"> 
        <div class="magnify">
         <a href="/wiki/File:1896_Olympic_opening_ceremony.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
        </div> The opening ceremony in the Panathinaiko Stadium
       </div> 
      </div> 
     </div> 
     <p>On 6 April (25 March according to the <a href="/wiki/Julian_calendar" title="Julian calendar">Julian calendar</a> then in use in Greece), the games of the First Olympiad were officially opened; it was <a href="/wiki/Easter_Monday" title="Easter Monday">Easter Monday</a> for both the <a href="/wiki/Western_Christianity" title="Western Christianity">Western</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Orthodox_Church" title="Eastern Orthodox Church">Eastern Christian Churches</a> and the anniversary of <a href="/wiki/Greek_War_of_Independence" title="Greek War of Independence">Greece's independence</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> The Panathinaiko Stadium was filled with an estimated 80,000 spectators, including King <a href="/wiki/George_I_of_Greece" title="George I of Greece">George I of Greece</a>, his wife <a href="/wiki/Olga_Konstantinova_of_Russia" title="Olga Konstantinova of Russia" class="mw-redirect">Olga</a>, and their sons. Most of the competing athletes were aligned on the infield, grouped by nation. After a speech by the president of the organising committee, Crown Prince Constantine, his father officially opened the Games:<sup id="cite_ref-IOCA_26-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IOCA-26">[26]</a></sup></p> 
     <blockquote> 
      <p>"I declare the opening of the first international Olympic Games in Athens. Long live the Nation. Long live the Greek people."</p> 
     </blockquote> 
     <p>Afterwards, nine bands and 150 choir singers performed an <a href="/wiki/Olympic_Hymn" title="Olympic Hymn">Olympic Hymn</a>, composed by <a href="/wiki/Spyridon_Samaras" title="Spyridon Samaras">Spyridon Samaras</a>, with words by poet <a href="/wiki/Kostis_Palamas" title="Kostis Palamas">Kostis Palamas</a>. Thereafter, a variety of musical offerings provided the backgrounds to the Opening Ceremonies until <a href="/wiki/1960_Summer_Olympics" title="1960 Summer Olympics">1960</a>, since which time the Samaras/Palamas composition has become the official Olympic Anthem (decision taken by the IOC Session in 1958). Other elements of current Olympic opening ceremonies were initiated later: the <a href="/wiki/Olympic_flame" title="Olympic flame">Olympic flame</a> was first lit in <a href="/wiki/1928_Summer_Olympics" title="1928 Summer Olympics">1928</a>, the first athletes' oath was sworn at the <a href="/wiki/1920_Summer_Olympics" title="1920 Summer Olympics">1920 Summer Olympics</a>, and the first officials' oath was taken at the <a href="/wiki/1972_Summer_Olympics" title="1972 Summer Olympics">1972 Olympic Games</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-IOCA_26-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IOCA-26">[26]</a></sup></p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Events">Events</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1896_Summer_Olympics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Events">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>At the 1894 Sorbonne congress, a large roster of sports were suggested for the program in Athens. The first official announcements regarding the sporting events to be held featured sports such as football and cricket, but these plans were never finalised, and these sports did not make the final list for the Games. <a href="/wiki/Sport_rowing" title="Sport rowing" class="mw-redirect">Rowing</a> and <a href="/wiki/Yachting" title="Yachting">yachting</a> were scheduled, but had to be cancelled due to poor weather on the planned day of competition.<sup id="cite_ref-C98-99_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-C98-99-27">[27]</a></sup> As a result, the 1896 Summer Olympics programme featured 9 sports encompassing 10 disciplines and 43 events. The number of events in each discipline is noted in parentheses.</p>

     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Athletics">Athletics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1896_Summer_Olympics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Athletics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <div role="note" class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article: 
      <a href="/wiki/Athletics_at_the_1896_Summer_Olympics" title="Athletics at the 1896 Summer Olympics">Athletics at the 1896 Summer Olympics</a>
     </div> 
     <p>The athletics events had the most international field of any of the sports. The major highlight was the <a href="/wiki/Athletics_at_the_1896_Summer_Olympics_%E2%80%93_Men%27s_marathon" title="Athletics at the 1896 Summer Olympics – Men's marathon">marathon</a>, held for the first time in international competition. <a href="/wiki/Spyridon_Louis" title="Spyridon Louis">Spyridon Louis</a>, a previously unrecognised water carrier, won the event to become the only Greek athletics champion and a national hero. Although Greece had been favoured to win the <a href="/wiki/Athletics_at_the_1896_Summer_Olympics_%E2%80%93_Men%27s_discus_throw" title="Athletics at the 1896 Summer Olympics – Men's discus throw">discus</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Athletics_at_the_1896_Summer_Olympics_%E2%80%93_Men%27s_shot_put" title="Athletics at the 1896 Summer Olympics – Men's shot put">shot put</a>, the best Greek athletes finished just behind the American <a href="/wiki/Robert_Garrett" title="Robert Garrett">Robert Garrett</a> in both events.<sup id="cite_ref-Y153_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Y153-3">[3]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>No <a href="/wiki/World_record" title="World record">world records</a> were set, as few top international competitors had elected to compete. In addition, the curves of the track were very tight, making fast times in the running events virtually impossible. Despite this, <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Burke_(athlete)" title="Thomas Burke (athlete)">Thomas Burke</a>, of the United States, won the <a href="/wiki/Athletics_at_the_1896_Summer_Olympics_%E2%80%93_Men%27s_100_metres" title="Athletics at the 1896 Summer Olympics – Men's 100 metres">100-meter</a> race in 12.0 seconds and the <a href="/wiki/Athletics_at_the_1896_Summer_Olympics_%E2%80%93_Men%27s_400_metres" title="Athletics at the 1896 Summer Olympics – Men's 400 metres">400-meter</a> race in 54.2 seconds. Burke was the only one who used the "<a href="/wiki/Crouch_start" title="Crouch start" class="mw-redirect">crouch start</a>" (putting his knee on soil), confusing the jury. Eventually, he was allowed to start from this "uncomfortable position".<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>Australian competitor <a href="/wiki/Edwin_Flack" title="Edwin Flack">Edwin Flack</a> came to Athens to watch the games, but decided to compete in the athletics events. He won two races—the <a href="/wiki/Athletics_at_the_1896_Summer_Olympics_%E2%80%93_Men%27s_800_metres" title="Athletics at the 1896 Summer Olympics – Men's 800 metres">800-meter</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Athletics_at_the_1896_Summer_Olympics_%E2%80%93_Men%27s_1500_metres" title="Athletics at the 1896 Summer Olympics – Men's 1500 metres">1500-meter</a>.</p>

     <p><a href="/wiki/Chile" title="Chile">Chile</a> claims one athlete, <a href="/wiki/Luis_Subercaseaux" title="Luis Subercaseaux">Luis Subercaseaux</a>, competed for the nation at the 1896 Summer Olympics. This makes Chile one of the 14 nations to appear at the inaugural Summer Olympic Games. Subercaseaux's results are not listed in the Official Report, though that Report typically includes only winners and Subercaseaux won no medals.<sup id="cite_ref-olimpismo2007.blogspot.com_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-olimpismo2007.blogspot.com-29">[29]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-olimpismo2007.blogspot.com_29-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-olimpismo2007.blogspot.com-29">[29]</a></sup> Recently, a study commissioned to Chilean forensic police (<a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.investigaciones.cl/">PDI</a>) decided (by studies of facial recognition), that Subercaseaux would be the participant in a <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.spherasports.com/files/20140411FA-2.jpg">famous photo</a> of 100 meter's second series.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30">[30]</a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Cycling">Cycling</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1896_Summer_Olympics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Cycling">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <div role="note" class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article: 
      <a href="/wiki/Cycling_at_the_1896_Summer_Olympics" title="Cycling at the 1896 Summer Olympics">Cycling at the 1896 Summer Olympics</a>
     </div> 
     <p>The rules of the <a href="/wiki/International_Cycling_Association" title="International Cycling Association">International Cycling Association</a> were used for the cycling competitions.<sup id="cite_ref-LWC_31-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LWC-31">[31]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Track_cycling" title="Track cycling">track cycling</a> events were held at the newly built <a href="/wiki/Neo_Phaliron_Velodrome" title="Neo Phaliron Velodrome">Neo Phaliron Velodrome</a>. Only one road event was held, a race from Athens to Marathon and back (87 kilometres).</p> 
     <p>In the track events, the best cyclist was Frenchman <a href="/wiki/Paul_Masson_(cyclist)" title="Paul Masson (cyclist)">Paul Masson</a>, who won the one lap <a href="/wiki/Time_trial" title="Time trial">time trial</a>, the sprint event, and the 10,000 meters. In the 100 kilometres event, Masson entered as a pacemaker for his compatriot <a href="/wiki/L%C3%A9on_Flameng" title="Léon Flameng">Léon Flameng</a>. Flameng won the event, after a fall, and after stopping to wait for his Greek opponent <a href="/wiki/Georgios_Kolettis" title="Georgios Kolettis">Georgios Kolettis</a> to fix a mechanical problem. The Austrian fencer <a href="/wiki/Adolf_Schmal" title="Adolf Schmal">Adolf Schmal</a> won the 12-hour race, which was completed by only two cyclists, while the road race event was won by <a href="/wiki/Aristidis_Konstantinidis" title="Aristidis Konstantinidis">Aristidis Konstantinidis</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-LW_32-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LW-32">[32]</a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fencing">Fencing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1896_Summer_Olympics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Fencing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <div role="note" class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article: 
      <a href="/wiki/Fencing_at_the_1896_Summer_Olympics" title="Fencing at the 1896 Summer Olympics">Fencing at the 1896 Summer Olympics</a>
     </div> 
     <p>The fencing events were held in the <a href="/wiki/Zappeion" title="Zappeion">Zappeion</a>, which, built with money <a href="/wiki/Evangelis_Zappas" title="Evangelis Zappas" class="mw-redirect">Evangelis Zappas</a> had given to revive the ancient Olympic Games, had never seen any athletic contests before.<sup id="cite_ref-Y148_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Y148-33">[33]</a></sup> Unlike other sports (in which only amateurs were allowed to take part at the Olympics), professionals were allowed to compete in fencing, though in a separate event. These professionals were considered gentlemen athletes, just as the amateurs.<sup id="cite_ref-C46-47_24-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-C46-47-24">[24]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>Four events were scheduled, but the <a href="/wiki/%C3%89p%C3%A9e" title="Épée">épée</a> event was cancelled for unknown reasons. The <a href="/wiki/Fencing_at_the_1896_Summer_Olympics_%E2%80%93_Men%27s_foil" title="Fencing at the 1896 Summer Olympics – Men's foil">foil event</a> was won by a Frenchman, <a href="/wiki/Eug%C3%A8ne-Henri_Gravelotte" title="Eugène-Henri Gravelotte">Eugène-Henri Gravelotte</a>, who beat his countryman, <a href="/wiki/Henri_Callot" title="Henri Callot">Henri Callot</a>, in the final.<sup id="cite_ref-Y148_33-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Y148-33">[33]</a></sup> The other two events, the <a href="/wiki/Fencing_at_the_1896_Summer_Olympics_%E2%80%93_Men%27s_sabre" title="Fencing at the 1896 Summer Olympics – Men's sabre">sabre</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Fencing_at_the_1896_Summer_Olympics_%E2%80%93_Men%27s_masters_foil" title="Fencing at the 1896 Summer Olympics – Men's masters foil">masters foil</a>, were won by Greek fencers. <a href="/wiki/Leonidas_Pyrgos" title="Leonidas Pyrgos">Leonidas Pyrgos</a>, who won the latter event, became the first Greek Olympic champion in the modern era.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Gymnastics">Gymnastics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1896_Summer_Olympics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Gymnastics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <div role="note" class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article: 
      <a href="/wiki/Gymnastics_at_the_1896_Summer_Olympics" title="Gymnastics at the 1896 Summer Olympics">Gymnastics at the 1896 Summer Olympics</a>
     </div> 
     <p>The gymnastics competition was carried out on the infield of the Panathinaiko Stadium. Germany had sent an 11-man team, which won five of the eight events, including both team events. In the team event on the <a href="/wiki/Gymnastics_at_the_1896_Summer_Olympics_%E2%80%93_Men%27s_team_horizontal_bar" title="Gymnastics at the 1896 Summer Olympics – Men's team horizontal bar">horizontal bar</a>, the German team was unopposed. Three Germans added individual titles: <a href="/wiki/Hermann_Weing%C3%A4rtner" title="Hermann Weingärtner">Hermann Weingärtner</a> won the <a href="/wiki/Gymnastics_at_the_1896_Summer_Olympics_%E2%80%93_Men%27s_horizontal_bar" title="Gymnastics at the 1896 Summer Olympics – Men's horizontal bar">horizontal bar event</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Flatow" title="Alfred Flatow">Alfred Flatow</a> won the <a href="/wiki/Gymnastics_at_the_1896_Summer_Olympics_%E2%80%93_Men%27s_parallel_bars" title="Gymnastics at the 1896 Summer Olympics – Men's parallel bars">parallel bars</a>; and <a href="/wiki/Carl_Schuhmann" title="Carl Schuhmann">Carl Schuhmann</a>, who also competed successfully in wrestling, won the <a href="/wiki/Gymnastics_at_the_1896_Summer_Olympics_%E2%80%93_Men%27s_vault" title="Gymnastics at the 1896 Summer Olympics – Men's vault">vault</a>. <a href="/wiki/Louis_Zutter" title="Louis Zutter">Louis Zutter</a>, a Swiss gymnast, won the <a href="/wiki/Gymnastics_at_the_1896_Summer_Olympics_%E2%80%93_Men%27s_pommel_horse" title="Gymnastics at the 1896 Summer Olympics – Men's pommel horse">pommel horse</a>, while Greeks <a href="/wiki/Ioannis_Mitropoulos" title="Ioannis Mitropoulos">Ioannis Mitropoulos</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nikolaos_Andriakopoulos" title="Nikolaos Andriakopoulos">Nikolaos Andriakopoulos</a> were victorious in the <a href="/wiki/Gymnastics_at_the_1896_Summer_Olympics_%E2%80%93_Men%27s_rings" title="Gymnastics at the 1896 Summer Olympics – Men's rings">rings</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gymnastics_at_the_1896_Summer_Olympics_%E2%80%93_Men%27s_rope_climbing" title="Gymnastics at the 1896 Summer Olympics – Men's rope climbing">rope climbing</a> events, respectively.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sailing">Sailing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1896_Summer_Olympics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Sailing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <p>A regatta of sailing boats was on the program of the <b>Games of the First Olympiad</b> for 31 March 1896. However this event had to be given up.</p> 
     <p>The Official English report states:</p> 
     <blockquote class="templatequote"> 
      <p>The Regatta could not take place because some special boats embarkation had not been provided for.</p> 
      <div class="templatequotecite">
       <cite>—?Charalambos Annino</cite>
      </div> 
     </blockquote> 
     <p>The German version gives a bit more clues:</p> 
     <blockquote class="templatequote"> 
      <p>Die Wettkämpfe im Segeln wurden vereitelt, da man weder bei uns die besonderen Boote dafür besass, noch fremde Bewerber sich gemeldet hatten.</p> 
      <div class="templatequotecite">
       <cite>—?same source.</cite>
      </div> 
     </blockquote> 
     <p>So no boats available from Greece and no foreign entries. Thus that ends the first edition of <i>Sailing at the Summer Olympics</i>!</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Shooting">Shooting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1896_Summer_Olympics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Shooting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <div role="note" class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article: 
      <a href="/wiki/Shooting_at_the_1896_Summer_Olympics" title="Shooting at the 1896 Summer Olympics">Shooting at the 1896 Summer Olympics</a>
     </div> 
     <p>Held at a range at <a href="/wiki/Kallithea" title="Kallithea">Kallithea</a>, the shooting competition consisted of five events—two using a rifle and three with the <a href="/wiki/Pistol" title="Pistol">pistol</a>. The first event, <a href="/wiki/Shooting_at_the_1896_Summer_Olympics_%E2%80%93_Men%27s_200_metre_military_rifle" title="Shooting at the 1896 Summer Olympics – Men's 200 metre military rifle">the military rifle</a>, was won by <a href="/wiki/Pantelis_Karasevdas" title="Pantelis Karasevdas">Pantelis Karasevdas</a>, the only competitor to hit the target with all of his shots. The second event, for <a href="/wiki/Shooting_at_the_1896_Summer_Olympics_%E2%80%93_Men%27s_25_metre_military_pistol" title="Shooting at the 1896 Summer Olympics – Men's 25 metre military pistol">military pistols</a>, was dominated by two American brothers: <a href="/wiki/John_Paine_(sharpshooter)" title="John Paine (sharpshooter)" class="mw-redirect">John</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sumner_Paine" title="Sumner Paine">Sumner Paine</a> became the first siblings to finish first and second in the same event. To avoid embarrassing their hosts, the brothers decided that only one of them would compete in the next pistol event, the <a href="/wiki/Shooting_at_the_1896_Summer_Olympics_%E2%80%93_Men%27s_30_metre_free_pistol" title="Shooting at the 1896 Summer Olympics – Men's 30 metre free pistol">free pistol</a>. Sumner Paine won that event, thereby becoming the first relative of an Olympic champion to become Olympic champion himself.<sup id="cite_ref-CP76.2C83_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CP76.2C83-35">[35]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>The Paine brothers did not compete in the <a href="/wiki/Shooting_at_the_1896_Summer_Olympics_%E2%80%93_Men%27s_25_metre_rapid_fire_pistol" title="Shooting at the 1896 Summer Olympics – Men's 25 metre rapid fire pistol">25-meter pistol event</a>, as the event judges determined that their weapons were not of the required calibre. In their absence, <a href="/wiki/Ioannis_Phrangoudis" title="Ioannis Phrangoudis" class="mw-redirect">Ioannis Phrangoudis</a> won. The final event, the <a href="/wiki/Shooting_at_the_1896_Summer_Olympics_%E2%80%93_Men%27s_300_metre_free_rifle,_three_positions" title="Shooting at the 1896 Summer Olympics – Men's 300 metre free rifle, three positions">free rifle</a>, began on the same day. However, the event could not be completed due to darkness and was finalised the next morning, when <a href="/wiki/Georgios_Orphanidis" title="Georgios Orphanidis">Georgios Orphanidis</a> was crowned the champion.<sup id="cite_ref-CP76.2C83_35-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CP76.2C83-35">[35]</a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Swimming">Swimming</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1896_Summer_Olympics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Swimming">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <div role="note" class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article: 
      <a href="/wiki/Swimming_at_the_1896_Summer_Olympics" title="Swimming at the 1896 Summer Olympics">Swimming at the 1896 Summer Olympics</a>
     </div> 
     <div class="thumb tleft"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:Hajos.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Hajos.jpg/220px-Hajos.jpg" width="220" height="334" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/18/Hajos.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/18/Hajos.jpg 2x" data-file-width="246" data-file-height="374"></a> 
       <div class="thumbcaption"> 
        <div class="magnify">
         <a href="/wiki/File:Hajos.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
        </div> 
        <a href="/wiki/Alfr%C3%A9d_Haj%C3%B3s" title="Alfréd Hajós">Alfréd Hajós</a>, the first Olympic champion in swimming, is one of only two Olympians to have won medals in both sport and art competitions
       </div> 
      </div> 
     </div> 
     <p>The swimming competition was held in the open sea because the organizers had refused to spend the money necessary for a specially constructed stadium. Nearly 20,000 spectators lined the <a href="/wiki/Bay_of_Zea" title="Bay of Zea">Bay of Zea</a> off the <a href="/wiki/Piraeus" title="Piraeus">Piraeus</a> coast to watch the events. The water in the bay was cold, and the competitors suffered during their races. There were three open events (<a href="/wiki/Swimming_at_the_1896_Summer_Olympics_%E2%80%93_Men%27s_100_metre_freestyle" title="Swimming at the 1896 Summer Olympics – Men's 100 metre freestyle">men's 100-metre freestyle</a>, <a href="/wiki/Swimming_at_the_1896_Summer_Olympics_%E2%80%93_Men%27s_500_metre_freestyle" title="Swimming at the 1896 Summer Olympics – Men's 500 metre freestyle">men's 500-metre freestyle</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Swimming_at_the_1896_Summer_Olympics_%E2%80%93_Men%27s_1200_metre_freestyle" title="Swimming at the 1896 Summer Olympics – Men's 1200 metre freestyle">men's 1200 metre freestyle</a>), in addition to a <a href="/wiki/Swimming_at_the_1896_Summer_Olympics_%E2%80%93_Men%27s_sailors_100_metre_freestyle" title="Swimming at the 1896 Summer Olympics – Men's sailors 100 metre freestyle">special event open only to Greek sailors</a>, all of which were held on the same day (11 April).<sup id="cite_ref-LW_32-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LW-32">[32]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>For <a href="/wiki/Alfr%C3%A9d_Haj%C3%B3s" title="Alfréd Hajós">Alfréd Hajós</a> of Hungary, this meant he could only compete in two of the events, as they were held too close together, which made it impossible for him to adequately recuperate. Nevertheless, he won the two events in which he swam, the 100 and 1200 meter <a href="/wiki/Freestyle_swimming" title="Freestyle swimming">freestyle</a>. Hajós later became one of only two Olympians to win a medal in both the athletic and <a href="/wiki/Art_competitions_at_the_Summer_Olympics" title="Art competitions at the Summer Olympics">artistic competitions</a>, when he won a silver medal for architecture in 1924. The 500-meter freestyle was won by Austrian swimmer <a href="/wiki/Paul_Neumann_(swimmer)" title="Paul Neumann (swimmer)">Paul Neumann</a>, who defeated his opponents by more than a minute and a half.</p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tennis">Tennis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1896_Summer_Olympics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Tennis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <div role="note" class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article: 
      <a href="/wiki/Tennis_at_the_1896_Summer_Olympics" title="Tennis at the 1896 Summer Olympics">Tennis at the 1896 Summer Olympics</a>
     </div> 
     <p>Although tennis was already a major sport by the end of the 19th century, none of the top players turned up for the tournament in Athens. The competition was held at the courts of the <a href="/wiki/Athens_Lawn_Tennis_Club" title="Athens Lawn Tennis Club">Athens Lawn Tennis Club</a>, and the infield of the velodrome used for the cycling events. <a href="/wiki/John_Pius_Boland" title="John Pius Boland">John Pius Boland</a>, who won the event, had been entered in the competition by a fellow-student of his at Oxford; the Greek, Konstantinos Manos. As a member of the Athens Lawn Tennis sub-committee, Manos had been trying, with the assistance of Boland, to recruit competitors for the Athens Games from among the sporting circles of Oxford University. In the first round, Boland defeated <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Traun" title="Friedrich Traun">Friedrich Traun</a>, a promising tennis player from <a href="/wiki/Hamburg" title="Hamburg">Hamburg</a>, who had been eliminated in the 100-meter sprint competition. Boland and Traun decided to team up for the doubles event, in which they reached the final and defeated their Greek and Egyptian opponents after losing the first set.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Weightlifting">Weightlifting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1896_Summer_Olympics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Weightlifting">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <div role="note" class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article: 
      <a href="/wiki/Weightlifting_at_the_1896_Summer_Olympics" title="Weightlifting at the 1896 Summer Olympics">Weightlifting at the 1896 Summer Olympics</a>
     </div> 
     <div class="thumb tright"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:Launceston_Elliot.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/Launceston_Elliot.jpg/220px-Launceston_Elliot.jpg" width="220" height="332" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/0/05/Launceston_Elliot.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/0/05/Launceston_Elliot.jpg 2x" data-file-width="265" data-file-height="400"></a> 
       <div class="thumbcaption"> 
        <div class="magnify">
         <a href="/wiki/File:Launceston_Elliot.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
        </div> 
        <a href="/wiki/Launceston_Elliot" title="Launceston Elliot">Launceston Elliot</a>, winner of the one-armed weightlifting event, was popular with the Greek audience, who found him very handsome
       </div> 
      </div> 
     </div> 
     <p>The sport of weightlifting was still young in 1896, and the rules differed from those in use today. Competitions were held outdoors, in the infield of the <a href="/wiki/Panathenaic_Stadium" title="Panathenaic Stadium">main stadium</a>, and there were no weight limits. The first event was held in a style now known as the "<a href="/wiki/Clean_and_jerk" title="Clean and jerk">clean and jerk</a>". Two competitors stood out: Scotsman <a href="/wiki/Launceston_Elliot" title="Launceston Elliot">Launceston Elliot</a> and <a href="/wiki/Viggo_Jensen" title="Viggo Jensen">Viggo Jensen</a> of Denmark. Both of them lifted the same weight; but the jury, with Prince George as the chairman, ruled that Jensen had done so in a better style. The British delegation, unfamiliar with this tie-breaking rule, lodged a protest. The lifters were eventually allowed to make further attempts, but neither lifter improved, and Jensen was declared the champion.<sup id="cite_ref-C70-71_37-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-C70-71-37">[37]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>Elliot got his revenge in the <a href="/wiki/Weightlifting_at_the_1896_Summer_Olympics_%E2%80%93_Men%27s_one_hand_lift" title="Weightlifting at the 1896 Summer Olympics – Men's one hand lift">one hand lift</a> event, which was held immediately after the two-handed one. Jensen had been slightly injured during his last two-handed attempt, and was no match for Elliot, who won the competition easily. The Greek audience was charmed by the Scottish victor, whom they considered very attractive. A curious incident occurred during the weightlifting event: a servant was ordered to remove the weights, which appeared to be a difficult task for him. Prince George came to his assistance; he picked up the weight and threw it a considerable distance with ease, to the delight of the crowd.<sup id="cite_ref-C70-71_37-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-C70-71-37">[37]</a></sup></p> 
     <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Wrestling">Wrestling</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1896_Summer_Olympics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Wrestling">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3> 
     <div role="note" class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">
      Main article: 
      <a href="/wiki/Wrestling_at_the_1896_Summer_Olympics" title="Wrestling at the 1896 Summer Olympics">Wrestling at the 1896 Summer Olympics</a>
     </div> 
     <div class="thumb tleft"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:Schuhmann_lotta_atene_1896.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/Schuhmann_lotta_atene_1896.jpg/220px-Schuhmann_lotta_atene_1896.jpg" width="220" height="154" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/Schuhmann_lotta_atene_1896.jpg/330px-Schuhmann_lotta_atene_1896.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/Schuhmann_lotta_atene_1896.jpg/440px-Schuhmann_lotta_atene_1896.jpg 2x" data-file-width="629" data-file-height="440"></a> 
       <div class="thumbcaption"> 
        <div class="magnify">
         <a href="/wiki/File:Schuhmann_lotta_atene_1896.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a>
        </div> 
        <a href="/wiki/Carl_Schuhmann" title="Carl Schuhmann">Carl Schuhmann</a> (left) and 
        <a href="/wiki/Georgios_Tsitas" title="Georgios Tsitas">Georgios Tsitas</a> shake hands before the final match of the wrestling competition
       </div> 
      </div> 
     </div> 
     <p>No weight classes existed for the wrestling competition, held in the Panathinaiko Stadium, which meant that there would only be one winner among competitors of all sizes. The rules used were similar to modern <a href="/wiki/Greco-Roman_wrestling" title="Greco-Roman wrestling">Greco-Roman wrestling</a>, although there was no time limit, and not all leg holds were forbidden (in contrast to current rules).</p> 
     <p>Apart from the two Greek contestants, all the competitors had previously been active in other sports. Weightlifting champion Launceston Elliot faced gymnastics champion <a href="/wiki/Carl_Schuhmann" title="Carl Schuhmann">Carl Schuhmann</a>. The latter won and advanced into the final, where he met <a href="/wiki/Georgios_Tsitas" title="Georgios Tsitas">Georgios Tsitas</a>, who had previously defeated <a href="/wiki/Stephanos_Christopoulos" title="Stephanos Christopoulos">Stephanos Christopoulos</a>. Darkness forced the final match to be suspended after 40 minutes; it was continued the following day, when Schuhmann needed only fifteen minutes to finish the bout.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup></p> 
     <div style="clear:both;"></div> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Closing_ceremony">Closing ceremony</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1896_Summer_Olympics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Closing ceremony">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>On the morning of Sunday 12 April (3 April, according to Julian calendar then used in Greece) King George the great organised a banquet for officials and athletes (even though some competitions had not yet been held). During his speech, he made clear that, as far as he was concerned, the Olympics should be held in Athens permanently. The official closing ceremony was held the following Wednesday, after being postponed from Tuesday due to rain. Again the royal family attended the ceremony, which was opened by the <a href="/wiki/National_anthem_of_Greece" title="National anthem of Greece" class="mw-redirect">national anthem of Greece</a> and an ode composed in <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">ancient Greek</a> by <a href="/wiki/George_S._Robertson" title="George S. Robertson">George S. Robertson</a>, a British athlete and scholar.<sup id="cite_ref-C50_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-C50-39">[39]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>Afterwards, the king awarded prizes to the winners. Unlike today, the first-place winners received silver medals, an olive branch and a diploma. Athletes who placed second received copper medals, a branch of laurel and a diploma. Third place winners did not receive a medal. Some winners also received additional prizes, such as Spyridon Louis, who received a cup from <a href="/wiki/Michel_Br%C3%A9al" title="Michel Bréal">Michel Bréal</a>, a friend of Coubertin, who had conceived the marathon event. Louis then led the medalists on a lap of honour around the stadium, while the Olympic Hymn was played again. The King then formally announced that the first Olympiad was at an end, and left the Stadium, while the band played the Greek national hymn and the crowd cheered.<sup id="cite_ref-C50_39-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-C50-39">[39]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>Like the Greek king, many others supported the idea of holding the next Games in Athens; most of the American competitors signed a letter to the Crown Prince expressing this wish. Coubertin, however, was heavily opposed to this idea, as he envisioned international rotation as one of the cornerstones of the modern Olympics. According to his wish, the next Games were held in Paris, although they would be somewhat overshadowed by the concurrently held <a href="/wiki/Exposition_Universelle_(1900)" title="Exposition Universelle (1900)">Universal Exposition</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">[40]</a></sup></p> 
     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Participating_nations">Participating nations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1896_Summer_Olympics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Participating nations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <div class="thumb tright"> 
      <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
       <a href="/wiki/File:1896_Olympic_games_countries.PNG" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/1896_Olympic_games_countries.PNG/220px-1896_Olympic_games_countries.PNG" width="220" height="96" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/1896_Olympic_games_countries.PNG/330px-1896_Olympic_games_countries.PNG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/1896_Olympic_games_countries.PNG/440px-1896_Olympic_games_countries.PNG 2x" data-file-width="1425" data-file-height="625"></a>

     <p>The concept of national teams was not a major part of the Olympic movement until the <a href="/wiki/1906_Intercalated_Games" title="1906 Intercalated Games">Intercalated Games</a> 10 years later, though many sources list the nationality of competitors in 1896 and give medal counts. There are significant conflicts with regard to which nations competed. The International Olympic Committee gives a figure of 14, but does not list them.<sup id="cite_ref-IOCA_26-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-IOCA-26">[26]</a></sup> The following 14 are most likely the ones recognised by the IOC. Some sources list 12, excluding Chile and Bulgaria; others list 13, including those two but excluding Italy. Egypt is also sometimes included because of <a href="/wiki/Dionysios_Kasdaglis" title="Dionysios Kasdaglis">Dionysios Kasdaglis</a>' participation. Belgium and Russia had entered the names of competitors, but withdrew.</p>

     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Medal_count">Medal count</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1896_Summer_Olympics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Medal count">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <div role="note" class="hatnote">

     <p>Ten of the 14 participating nations earned medals, in addition to three medals won by <a href="/wiki/Mixed_team_at_the_Olympics" title="Mixed team at the Olympics" class="mw-redirect">mixed teams</a>, i.e. teams made up of athletes from multiple nations. The United States won the most gold medals* (11), while host nation Greece won the most medals overall (46) as well as the most silver* (17) and bronze* (19) medals, finishing with one fewer gold medal than the United States.<sup id="cite_ref-medal_count_48-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-medal_count-48">[48]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>During these inaugural Olympics, winners were given a silver medal, an <a href="/wiki/Olive" title="Olive">olive</a> branch, and a diploma, while runners-up received a copper medal, <a href="/wiki/Bay_Laurel" title="Bay Laurel" class="mw-redirect">laurel</a> branch, and diploma.<sup id="cite_ref-official_report_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-official_report-51">[51]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">[52]</a></sup> The IOC has retroactively assigned gold, silver and bronze medals to the three best placed athletes in each event to comport with more recent traditions.<sup id="cite_ref-medal_count_48-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-medal_count-48">[48]</a></sup></p> 
     <p>*As stated in the previous paragraph, it should be noted that the use of 'Gold medals' did not occur until the 1904 St. Louis, Missouri Olympic Games to represent a First place finish. The 'Silver medal' did not represent Second place until the 1904 Games. In 1896, Silver was given to the winner. 'Bronze medals' were not given for Third place initially. In 1896, they were given to the Second place ("runner-up") finisher. In 1904, they were first given as Third place medals ("second runner-up").<sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">[53]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup></p>

     <h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Female_competitors">Female competitors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=1896_Summer_Olympics&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Female competitors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2> 
     <p>Women were not allowed to compete at the 1896 Summer Olympics, because de Coubertin felt that their inclusion would be "impractical, uninteresting, unaesthetic and incorrect".<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> However, one woman, <a href="/wiki/Stamata_Revithi" title="Stamata Revithi">Stamata Revithi</a>, ran the marathon course on 11 April, the day after the men had run the official race. Although she was not allowed to enter the stadium at the end of her race, Revithi finished the marathon in about five hours and 30 minutes, and found witnesses to sign their names and verify the starting and finishing times. Revithi intended to present this documentation to the <a href="/wiki/Hellenic_Olympic_Committee" title="Hellenic Olympic Committee">Hellenic Olympic Committee</a>, hoping that they would recognise her achievement. Neither her reports nor documents from the Hellenic Olympic Committee have been discovered to provide corroboration.<sup id="cite_ref-Rev_58-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rev-58">[58]</a></sup></p>
